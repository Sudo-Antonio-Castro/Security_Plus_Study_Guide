Data Classification and Compliance

Data protection begins with knowing what kind of information you hold, how sensitive it is, and what rules apply to it.

Core Ideas

  • Classifications such as public, private, sensitive, confidential, proprietary, PII, PHI, and financial data guide control selection.
  • Compliance often requires encryption, retention safeguards, access controls, and breach handling procedures.

Support Focus

  • Data lifecycle thinking improves storage, sharing, retention, and destruction decisions.
  • Human-readable and non-human-readable data create different monitoring challenges.
Summary

The right controls depend on the data type, legal context, and business value involved.

Security+ Exam Tip

Classification is the foundation that lets DLP and handling rules make sense.

Personnel Policies

People are part of the security system, so policies and training must shape behavior before risky habits become incidents.

Core Ideas

  • AUPs, codes of conduct, clean desk rules, device restrictions, and role-based training all support secure operations.
  • Security awareness works best when it is continuous, relevant, and measured over time.

Support Focus

  • Train users to recognize phishing, risky behavior, and reporting obligations.
  • Policies should be enforceable, understandable, and aligned to actual job roles.
Summary

A mature security culture treats policy and training as operational controls, not paperwork.

Security+ Exam Tip

Users are not just a weakness to manage—they are also a detection surface and a reporting resource.
