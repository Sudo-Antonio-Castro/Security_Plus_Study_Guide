Incident Response

Incident response follows a repeatable lifecycle from preparation and identification through containment, eradication, recovery, and lessons learned.

Core Ideas

  • Containment choices depend on risk, evidence preservation, and business impact.
  • Recovery is not complete until systems are monitored for recurrence and documentation is complete.

Support Focus

  • Preparation includes tooling, people, plans, and communications.
  • Containment, eradication, and recovery may need to loop multiple times.
Summary

Good response is coordinated, documented, and prioritized under pressure.

Security+ Exam Tip

Containment limits scope; eradication removes the cause; recovery restores operations.

Digital Forensics

Forensics preserves and analyzes evidence so responders can understand what happened without destroying trace data.

Core Ideas

  • Volatile data often needs to be captured before power is removed.
  • Chain of custody and validated acquisition matter whenever evidence may support legal or disciplinary action.

Support Focus

  • Use the right collection method for memory, disks, and logs.
  • Protect evidence integrity while still supporting response needs.
Summary

Forensics supports both incident handling and defensible post-incident analysis.

Security+ Exam Tip

If the scenario mentions legal action or evidence handling, chain of custody matters.

Data Sources

Responders depend on network, endpoint, application, cloud, and identity logs to build a timeline of malicious activity.

Core Ideas

  • Different logs answer different questions about users, hosts, traffic, and processes.
  • Packet captures and flow data provide different levels of depth and storage cost.

Support Focus

  • Correlate alerts with raw logs or packet data when needed.
  • Time synchronization is essential when comparing sources.
Summary

Log diversity improves visibility, but only if collection and retention are planned.

Security+ Exam Tip

A SIEM can correlate data, but it still depends on the quality of the underlying sources.

Alerting and Monitoring Tools

Security teams rely on SIEM, SOAR, IDS/IPS, EDR, dashboards, and playbooks to prioritize and automate response work.

Core Ideas

  • Monitoring should reduce time to detection without burying analysts in noise.
  • Playbooks convert common incident patterns into repeatable operational steps.

Support Focus

  • Know the difference between orchestration, automation, and raw alerting.
  • Tuning thresholds and use cases is part of building a useful program.
Summary

Monitoring is most effective when it is tied to human-readable decisions and action.

Security+ Exam Tip

If a question asks about automating workflows between tools, think SOAR.
